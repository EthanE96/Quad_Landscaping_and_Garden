FEATURED PLANTS OF THE MONTH

Winterberry Holly

Winterberry holly is native to the entire eastern half of North America. It can be found growing from Nova Scotia to Florida in bogs, swamps, damp thickets, low areas, and along ponds and streams. In wet sites in the wild, it may form large thickets or colonies from suckers.

Camellias

Queens of the winter flowers, Camellias are attractive evergreen shrubs that are highly prized for the beauty of their exquisite blooms, their splendid evergreen foliage and their compact shapely habit.

Japanese Maples

Japanese maple is a small deciduous tree or large shrub with a broadly spreading crown. The plant can be grown as a small single-stemmed tree or large multiple stemmed shrub.

Most Popular Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I water my plants?

The general rule is that you should water your plants once or twice a week, using enough water to moisten the soil to a depth of about 6 inches each time.

How often should I fertilize my plants?

When and how often you need to fertilize your plants largely depends on the type of soil that the plants are rooted in. It is typically recommended to use two to three pounds of fertilizer for every 100 square feet of garden area.

What are the easiest vegetables to grow?

Lettuce, spinach, greenbeans, and cucumbers are widely thought to be the easiest vegetables to grow

What are the best houseplants for beginners?

We reccomend starting with a spider plant, a zz plant, or a snake plant if you are new to gardening!

Any advice on spending less money on plants?

Besides saving seeds and dividing perennials in late spring, take cuttings of annuals and tender perennials in the fall and root them in water over the winter.

What type of soil should I use for replotting?

Most plants grow perfectly fine in regular plotting soil. On some occasions, you might want to add some grit such as perlite or sand for extra drainage.

How can I prevent or deal with flies and fungus gnats?

One of the things you can do is to simply use a fork to rake the top level of the soil once or twice a week. In case you notice that you have an infestation, spray your plant with some Neem Oil.

Why are my plants' leaves leaves drooping or yellowing?

If you see that there are some leaves yellowing, but there are new ones still growing, then this is just the natural process of aging. If you don't see any signs of new growth and the leaves are getting brown and drooping, this may mean that you are overwatering.

Garden Maintenance Tips and Tricks

Plan Ahead

It can be easy to be discouraged when starting a garden, but don't worry. Everyone runs into this when starting a garden. Begin by making a plan. Answer a few questions for yourself. What plants am I interested in? Can those plants grow in my climate? How long do they take to grow? Where am I going to grow them at? Answering these questions will give you more of a concrete idea on what your garden will be.

Start Small

Even the biggest projects had the smallest beginnings. Starting a garden is already overwhelming. Start small to ensure you can give each of your plants the attention they need. You can always add plants to your garden in the future. Once you feel comfortable with maintaining your garden, you can grow it as you see fit.

Get Excited!

Gardening is an enjoyable hobby for many people. Choose plants that excite and interest you. Nothing is more satisfying than growing a fruit or vegetable you love and being able to taste it at the end. Goals like this to look forward to is what makes gardening fun. The beauty of the final result is enough to bring any gardener back for another cycle.

Break Ground!!!

The easiest way to become a successful gardener is to go out there and do it! There will be problems, there will be mistakes, there will be accidents. Do not let them discourage you. Remember that you are a beginner and that these things happen even to master gardeners. Get out there and show your plant some love. You wanted to start gardening for a reason, go out there and bring your goals to life.
